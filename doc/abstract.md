# Shallow cumulus clouds decrease in proportion to tropical overturning circulation

## Abstract

Shallow cumulus clouds affect on Earth's planetary albedo and cloud feedback, and explain climate model sensitivity to atmospheric CO$_2$. Previous studies have proposed that increased vertical mixing reduces cloud fraction by desiccating the relative humidity of the shallow cumulus layer. Others have argued correlations of clouds with cloud-base humidity and mass flux refute the dessication hypothesis.
Here we model the cloud mass and moisture flux 
from
the observed profile of humidity and 
the distribution of satellite cloud top height
[by fitting the distribution of the rate that clouds 
lose water to entrainment and precipitation].
Our model reproduces the mesoscale correlation of cloud fraction to 
the cloud base mass flux previously found 
over the northeastern tropical Atlantic Ocean in EUREC4A.
The moisture balance
between large-scale circulation drying and cloud moistening
serves as an emergent constraint on the shallow-cumulus regime
in the observed climate,
and predicts changes in clouds expected due to 
changes in the large-scale Hadley circulation 
due to changes in mean temperature.

## Introduction

The shallow cumulus regime covers XX% of Earth's oceans and strongly affects climate. Variation of shallow cumulus cloud feedback is the largest source of uncertainty in the climate sensitivity of climate models (Bony et al. 2005; Bony and Dufresne, 2006). Zelinka et al. (2025) distinguish the small and inconsisent changes in cloud radiative effects from the changes in the distribution of climate regimes over the planet, from the changes of the properties of clouds /within/ a climate regime, the largest of which is the positive feedbacks due to reduction of shallow cumulus shortwave cooling effect in the shallow cumulus regime.

Sherwood et al. (2014) proposed that in a warmed climate, increased vertical mixing reduces cloud fraction by desiccating the shallow cumulus layer. Field observations from EUREC4A (Stevens et al. 2021; George et al. 2022) show clouds are correlated more strongly to mass flux than to relative humidity, questioning the importance of humidity and its dessication by vertical mixing (Vogel et al. 2022). Furthermore, climate models that reproduce the observed mesoscale correlation of cloud fraction to cloud-base mass flux have weaker cloud radiative feedbacks and climate sensitivity (Vogel et al. 2022).

We propose that the shallow cumulus mass flux, and hence clouds will reduce in proportion to the reduction of mean tropical overturning circulation. The mean moisture balance over shallow convective regions provides another way to infer the effect of climate change on the clouds. In a steady climate, the moisture budget over the shallow cumulus regime is balanced, so the change in the drying by the sum of the large scale subsidence and advection compensates the moistening by the divergence of the shallow cumulus cloud eddy flux. 
The eddy flux by shallow cumulus ensemble is predicted from large scale drying by the tropical overturning circulation.

The large scale drying is due to the descending branch compensating deep convection in the mean tropical overturning circulation. The circulation drying the shallow cumulus region includes subsidence $W$ and horizontal wind $U$. Saturation specific humidity and tropospheric radiative-convective heat balance constrain the large scale tropical overturning ciruculation to about $d\ln W = d\ln U$ = 5 % per degree C of warming (Held and Soden, 2006). The mean vertical and horizontal motions multiplied by the humidity gradient generate dry advection. 
The large-scale moisture source $-W(\partial q /\partial z)$ and the cloud moisture flux $M(q_{cld} - q_{env})$ are both proportional to the humidity, which increases $\sim$7 % $K^{-1}$, but these compensating differences in the large-scale and cloud moisture fluxes predict low sensitivity of the cloud mass flux to humidity changes.

This balance applies in the shallow cumulus regime. We focus only on shallow cumulus clouds with tops below 4 km, compared to deeper convection in the intertropical convergence zone (ITCZ) and intermittent convergence, that is part of the upward branch of the mean tropical (Hadley-Walker) tropospheric circulation.

With a saturation humidity increase of $d\ln q=$ 7 % K$^{-1}$ and decrease $d\ln F_R = d\ln P = -2$ % K$^{-1}$ of tropospheric radiative divergence and mean precipitation, energetic constraints decrease the mean tropical circulation $d\ln W = d\ln U = -5$ % K$^{-1}$.
