# Abstract

Shallow cumulus clouds have been shown to have a strong effect on Earth's planetary albedo and cloud feedback.
Previous studies related the clouds to cloud-base humidity and mass flux.
Here we model the cloud mass and moisture flux 
from the observed profile of humidity
and the distribution of satellite cloud top height
by fitting the
distribution of the rate that clouds 
lose water to entrainment and precipitation.
Our model reproduces the mesoscale correlation of cloud fraction to 
the cloud base mass flux previously found 
over the northeastern tropical Atlantic Ocean in EUREC4A.
The moisture balance
between large-scale circulation drying and cloud moistening
serves as an emergent constraint on the shallow-cumulus regime
in the observed climate,
and predicts changes in clouds expected due to 
changes in the large-scale Hadley circulation 
due to changes in mean temperature.
This paradigm predicts low sensitivity to absolute humidity changes, because
an increase in cloud moisture flux from the cloud-environment humidity difference
compensates the increase in circulation drying due to the mean vertical humidity derivative.

# Introduction

The shallow cumulus regime covers XX% of Earth's oceans and strongly affects climate. Variation of shallow cumulus cloud feedback is the largest source of uncertainty in the climate sensitivity of climate models (Bony et al. 2005; Bony and Dufresne, 2006). Zelinka et al. (2025) distinguish the cloud radiative effects from the changes in the distribution of climate regimes, and changes of the properties of clouds /within/ a climate regime, showing that changes in the regimes small and inconsistent among models, and the largest positive feedbacks are due to cloud changes within the shallow cumulus regime.

Sherwood et al. (2014) proposed that increased vertical mixing reduces cloud fraction by desiccating the shallow cumulus layer. Field observations from EUREC4A (Stevens et al. 2021; George et al. 2022) show clouds are correlated more strongly to mass flux than to relative humidity, questioning the importance of humidity and its dessication by vertical mixing (Vogel et al. 2022). Furthermore, climate models that reproduce the observed mesoscale correlation of cloud fraction to cloud-base mass flux have weaker cloud radiative feedbacks, giving credence to their projections of relatively weak climate sensitivity (Vogel et al. 2022).
